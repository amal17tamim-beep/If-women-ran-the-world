A note before we begin

I want to be honest about where this came from. I was watching the news — the missiles, the rhetoric, the posturing between Israel, Iran, and the United States — and something in me snapped quietly. Not with rage, but with a kind of exhausted clarity.

I found myself thinking: we have tried this way, the way of dominance and deterrence and who-blinks-first, for a very long time now. And I started wondering, with genuine curiosity rather than naivety, what the alternative might actually look like. Not as a fantasy. As a serious question. This is my attempt to answer it honestly — which means celebrating what the evidence supports and confronting what it doesn't.

I Origin

The Experiment I Ran
as a Girl

I used to spend a lot of time on the playground figuring out how people work. Not intentionally — I was a kid — but looking back, I was running something close to a social experiment without knowing it.

When I played with other girls, something interesting happened. Before the game even started, we would plan it. Who would play which role, how the story would go, what we would do if someone got bored or felt left out. There was real sophistication in it. We were constantly reading the room — noticing if someone had gone quiet, adjusting so that everyone had a stake in it, making sure the afternoon actually worked for all of us.

Then I started playing with boys too, and I noticed something completely different. There was usually one kid — the coolest, the boldest, the one with that magnetic slightly-intimidating quality — and the game was essentially whatever he wanted it to be. The rest of us orbited him. Most of the time nobody said out loud that they didn't want to play that way. We just went along. Until someone didn't, and then there was a confrontation, a new kid became the center of gravity, and the whole cycle started over.

The girl group was quieter and more stable. The boy group was more dramatic and more brittle.

On two governance models, observed at age eight

I eventually spent more time with the girls — not because boys were bad, but because the structure made more sense to me. It was more interesting to build something together than to figure out where you ranked.

I have thought about that difference a lot since then. And what I kept coming back to, watching the news this week, is this: the men running the Middle East right now are playing the boy game. The question I want to ask is whether there is another game — and what it would actually look like.

II History & Anthropology

What a Matriarchy
Actually Is

Matriarchy is one of the most misused words in modern discourse. People assume it means "women in charge" — a mirror image of patriarchy with the genders swapped. It is more interesting and more complex than that.

The word comes from the Latin mater, meaning mother, and the Greek arkhein, to rule. In anthropological use, it describes societies organized through the female line — where descent, inheritance, land rights, and clan identity flow through women. These societies exist, they are not small or obscure, and they give us real data rather than speculation.

The Minangkabau of West Sumatra, Indonesia, are the world's largest matrilineal society — around 4.2 million people. Property and family names pass through women; clan mothers hold authority over land and mediate disputes. The Mosuo of southwestern China practice "walking marriage," in which women control households and children belong entirely to the mother's family. The Khasi of Meghalaya in northeast India organize inheritance, clan belonging, and political voice through the maternal line. These are not ancient relics. They are functioning, contemporary societies.

What is striking is not that women dominate men the way men have dominated women. It is the different quality of the authority — distributed rather than concentrated, relational rather than coercive, oriented toward continuity rather than conquest.

Anthropologists like Christopher Boehm have shown that hunter-gatherer societies were significantly more egalitarian than the agricultural civilizations that followed. The intensification of hierarchy appears to correlate with the accumulation of property and the need to establish paternity for inheritance. In other words: patriarchy may not be human nature. It may be a design choice that got locked in.

III Research & Evidence

What the Science
Actually Shows

On emotion and rationality

The oldest argument against women in leadership is that they are too emotional to make clear-headed decisions under pressure. I want to address this directly, because it deserves a serious response rather than a dismissal.

Women score higher on measures of emotional intelligence. A 2019 meta-analysis in Psychological Bulletin, covering 94 studies and over 15,000 participants, confirmed this across cultures. But emotional intelligence is not the opposite of rationality. Daniel Goleman's foundational Harvard research showed it is one of the strongest predictors of effective leadership — more predictive than IQ or technical expertise.

The institutions that nearly destroyed the global economy were not run by emotional women. They were run by men who were, in a very specific physiological sense, too emotional to think straight.

On the 2008 financial crisis and testosterone-driven risk-taking

On collective intelligence

In 2010, researchers at Carnegie Mellon published a study in Science measuring collective intelligence — the ability of groups to solve diverse problems together. The single strongest predictor was not the IQ of the smartest person in the room. It was the proportion of women in the group.

This is the girl-group dynamic I grew up watching, measured in a laboratory.

On national leadership

A 2021 study in The Lancet found that countries led by women during COVID-19 had significantly better health outcomes — lower death rates, faster policy responses — even after controlling for national wealth and population size.

The Nordic countries are the most sustained example. Norway, Sweden, Finland, Denmark, and Iceland consistently top global indices for quality of life, press freedom, and social trust. They have done it through flat hierarchies, strong institutional cooperation, and high female political participation. They are simply, measurably, among the best-governed places on earth.

IV Vision

What Such a System
Would Prioritize

Drawing on the research and the existing models, a genuinely matriarchal governance system would not be patriarchy with different faces at the top. It would prioritize fundamentally different things.

Long-term over short-term

Studies of female CEO decision-making show a consistent preference for longer investment horizons and lower risk tolerance. A 2018 study in the Journal of Corporate Finance found firms with female CEOs engaged significantly less in earnings management. Applied to governance: policies built around decades rather than election cycles — climate, infrastructure, education, public health.

Care as a political priority

The most transformative shift would be the revaluation of care work. Childcare, elder care, community maintenance — the labor that holds society together — is currently worth an estimated $10.8 trillion globally according to Oxfam, and almost none of it appears in economic statistics or political platforms. A matriarchal system would put care at the center of political life rather than the margins of it.

Coalition over domination

The dominant model of geopolitics is zero-sum. The matriarchal model would more likely treat security as something built through interdependence and reciprocal obligation. This is not naive — it is the logic that underlies the most successful international institution ever created. The European Union has kept peace on a continent that fought two world wars within thirty years, not through military dominance, but through shared institutions.

V The Honest Reckoning

What I Won't
Pretend Away

I want to be careful not to trade one mythology for another. An honest case for matriarchal governance has to confront the places where the evidence is uncomfortable.

Scale is a genuine problem

The most compelling matriarchal societies are relatively small and geographically contained. Consensus-based governance works beautifully at that scale. Whether it translates to a country of 330 million people, or to managing nuclear arsenals and global supply chains, is a genuinely open question.

Hard power is still real

Watching Israel, Iran, and the United States right now, I cannot pretend that good intentions are sufficient in a world where other actors read cooperation as vulnerability. I do not have a clean answer to this. I think it is a real problem.

Women are not a monolith

Margaret Thatcher governed as a hard-line authoritarian. Indira Gandhi declared a state of emergency and suspended democratic processes. A system that simply installs women in power does not automatically produce more empathetic governance. The goal is to redesign the structure itself — not just change who sits at the top.

Female competition is real too

I know this from the playground too. The girl group was more inclusive — but not without its own forms of hierarchy and exclusion. Relational aggression, social policing, the punishment of those who don't conform — these are documented patterns. A matriarchal system would need to reckon with them honestly.

VI Conclusion

What I
Actually Think

I started this piece looking at missiles and fire in the Middle East and asking myself a quiet, furious question: is there another way? The answer I have arrived at is yes — but not in the way the question might suggest.

The problem is not simply that men are in charge. The problem is that the entire architecture of modern power — concentrated authority, zero-sum competition, short-termism, the systematic devaluation of care and cooperation — was built around a particular set of values. Those values have produced extraordinary things: unprecedented wealth, scientific progress, a reduction in absolute poverty. They have also produced the situation I was watching on the news this week: brilliant, powerful people with enormous resources making everyone less safe.

The evidence consistently points toward a different set of organizing principles — distributed authority, long-term thinking, care as a genuine political priority, emotional intelligence as a strategic asset. Not because women are morally superior. But because these values have been systematically excluded from the rooms where decisions get made, and their absence has costs we are all now paying.

What I took from my childhood was not that girls are better than boys. What I took was that one way of organizing a group produces more stability, more genuine participation, and more durable outcomes than the other. The girl group I helped build was not perfect. But when someone new arrived, we found a place for them. When someone went quiet, we noticed. And we were still friends at the end of the afternoon.

"That seems worth building toward —
at any scale."
